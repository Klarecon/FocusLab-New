What could kill FocusLab at launch

A pre-mortem: it's six months from now and FocusLab failed. Here is every plausible reason why — ranked, with early-warning signs and what to do before launch day.

Prepared 27 June 2026 · Builds on the 9 risks in the GTM Brief — adds the product & legal risks that brief doesn't cover

00 How to read this

A pre-mortem is the opposite of a pitch. The pitch asks "how do we win?" — this asks "assume we lost; what was the cause?" Naming the cause now, while it's cheap, is the whole point.

The honest starting position (from your own brief): FocusLab is live, with zero monetization and zero awareness. That means the two biggest risks aren't competitors — they're (1) nobody shows up and (2) the people who do show up can't actually pay you, because today the app is a static site with no accounts and no payment. Most of what follows ladders up to those two.

Each risk below has a severity, a note on why it bites FocusLab specifically (not generic startup advice), an early-warning sign so you catch it before it's fatal, and a move. Risks marked new are not in your current GTM brief and are the ones I'd most want you to see.

01 The three ways it most likely dies

If FocusLab fails, it almost certainly fails one of these three deaths. Everything in the register is a contributing cause of one of them.

Death A — Silence

Nobody comes

The product is good but invisible. Product Hunt is a one-day blip, Reddit removes your posts as self-promo, the YouTube video gets 40 views. Traffic never reaches the few hundred visitors the funnel math needs. You're refining a tool no one is using.

Death B — Leak

They come, love it, leave — and can't pay

People finish the analyzer, get the "you waste 14 hours" hit… and bounce. No email, no payment, no return. Even the ones who want to pay hit a wall: there's no account and no checkout to take their money.

Death C — Shrug

One-and-done

They pay once, use it once, and churn in month two because it felt like a one-time quiz, not a tool. Or they clear their browser and lose the plan they paid for. MRR never compounds.

The rest of this document is the autopsy in advance — the specific, fixable causes behind A, B, and C.

02 The full risk register

Grouped by category. Severity reflects likelihood × damage at your stage — a "low" here can still be real, it just won't kill you first.

A · Product & technical foundation — the gaps unique to a static, account-less app
HighYou literally cannot charge anyone yet new

There is no auth, no login, no Stripe, no backend anywhere in the codebase — it's a static site with localStorage. A "Pro" tier means gating the Focus Table behind a payment, but there is no way to lock a feature to a paying user without accounts. A Stripe Payment Link takes money but can't unlock anything per-person.

Why it bitesYour whole revenue model (free Analyzer → paid Focus Table) assumes a paywall that doesn't exist. This is the #1 thing standing between you and your first dollar.
Warning signYou're "ready to launch paid" but the only mechanism is "I'll email you a link." That's not a business, that's a favor.
MoveDecide the auth path before the paid launch: cheapest is a hosted auth+billing layer (e.g. Clerk/Auth.js + Stripe, or an all-in-one like Outseta/Lemon Squeezy) gating the /focus route. Don't launch Pro until a stranger can pay and get access with zero manual steps from you.
HighUsers lose their work — no accounts, localStorage only new

The audit result and action plan live in localStorage under one browser. Clear cookies, switch from phone to laptop, use incognito, or get a new machine → the plan is gone. The "before/after dashboard" and "progress tracking" you're counting on for retention all depend on data that can vanish silently.

Why it bitesThe thing that makes someone stay subscribed (their saved plan, their progress) is exactly the thing that's most fragile. A paying user who loses their plan doesn't just churn — they feel cheated.
Warning signEarly testers say "wait, where did my plan go?" or can't open it on a second device.
MoveSame fix as above — accounts + server-side save are the real answer. Interim: a visible "your plan is saved only in this browser — email yourself a copy" export, so the data loss is at least honest and recoverable.
MedThe "aha number" feels invented new

The hook is "you waste ~14 hours a week." But that number is computed from the user's own self-reported sliders against static benchmarks. A skeptical manager can reverse-engineer that they basically told the tool the answer — which makes the headline feel like a horoscope, not a diagnosis.

Why it bitesYour category claim is "real diagnosis, not a vibes quiz." If the flagship number feels arbitrary, you become the exact thing you're positioning against.
Warning signComments like "isn't this just telling me what I put in?" or wildly different numbers from tiny input changes.
MoveShow your work: surface the benchmark each input is compared against ("managers in your role average X; you said Y"). Cite where benchmarks come from. Make the number feel earned, not generated.
B · Awareness & activation — Death A and the top of the funnel
HighThe free channels quietly don't work

The plan leans on Product Hunt + Reddit + YouTube + podcasts doing "the heavy lifting at $0." Each has a specific failure mode: PH is a single-day spike that fades; Reddit auto-removes promo and bans you for it; YouTube/podcast audiences take months to compound. "Free" is not the same as "reliable."

Why it bitesWith zero awareness and a thin paid budget ($300–500/mo), if the free channels underperform there's no fallback engine — the funnel starves at the top and every downstream metric looks broken for the wrong reason.
Warning sign<300 monthly visitors by week 4; Reddit posts removed; PH day generates a spike then flatlines to near-zero.
MoveTreat channels as experiments with a kill date, not a list to "do." Give value in Reddit/communities before linking (you already have the reddit-pain-miner work — use it to contribute, not just to post). Have one paid channel warm so you're never fully dependent on going viral.
HighYou have to teach the category before you can sell it

Your own competitor analysis says it: "Green field — but we'll have to teach the category." There is no search demand for "Pareto waste diagnostic." People search "time tracker" and "where does my time go" — terms that pattern-match you to RescueTime/Reclaim, exactly the box you're trying to escape.

Why it bitesCategory creation is the most expensive kind of marketing. A solo, under-funded launch is the worst-resourced way to attempt it. You can be right about the category and still run out of runway teaching it.
Warning signPeople describe you back to you wrong ("oh, it's a time tracker"); high bounce from search traffic that wanted tracking.
MoveDon't fight for new search terms early — ride existing demand and reframe on arrival ("not a tracker — your fix in 15 min, not 4 weeks"). Borrow Oren's authority and any coach's audience to teach the category for you.
C · Conversion & retention — Death B and Death C
HighChurn after month 1 (the brief's own #1)

FocusLab is shaped like a one-time event: take audit → get plan → done. The retention hooks (monthly re-assessment, progress tracking, before/after) are listed as features but not yet built — and they depend on the account/data layer that also doesn't exist yet.

Why it bitesA $29/mo product that delivers its full value in one sitting has no honest reason to bill a second month. Annual plans hide churn; they don't fix it.
Warning signLogins drop to near-zero after day 1; month-2 retention <40%; refund requests citing "I already got what I needed."
MoveBuild at least one recurring-value loop before charging monthly. If you can't yet, lead with the annual plan (one decision, value-led) rather than monthly (re-decided every 30 days against a flat usage curve).
MedConversion lands under 2%

The model needs 3–5% of completers to pay. But you're giving away the emotionally powerful half (the diagnosis) and charging for the less-dramatic half (the fix plan). Many users will feel "done" at the free aha-moment and never feel the pull to pay.

Why it bitesThe free tier is so satisfying it may cannibalize the paid one. The peak emotion is in the free product; the paid product is "work."
Warning signHigh completion + high email capture but <2% pay; heatmaps show people leaving right at the paywall.
MoveTest the gate placement (show a teaser of the fix plan so the value is visible before the wall), and test price ($19 vs $29). Make the paid side feel like relief from the problem you just made them feel, not extra homework.
D · Founder & dependency risk — single points of failure
MedThe coach track is one person's network new

The entire coach channel — your fastest path to $1K MRR (7 coaches = $1K) — runs through Oren's relationships and Oren's validation. If Oren gets busy, loses interest, or his network doesn't bite, that whole track evaporates and there's no second door into coaches.

Why it bitesIt's also a methodology dependency: the Pareto/Focus methodology that is the product is Oren's. Key-person risk on both distribution and credibility.
Warning signCoach conversations keep slipping; "verbal yeses," no prepays; you're waiting on one person to unblock the channel.
MoveYou already raised the bar correctly (3+ prepay, not verbal). Keep the individual track fully independent of Oren so no single person's availability can stall revenue. Start building a second coach-sourcing path (a community, a directory) in parallel.
MedYou're the only builder, and the build list is growing new

Auth, billing, accounts, retention loops, a landing page, and a marketing launch — all on one non-technical founder working through an AI assistant. The pre-launch must-build list (below) is genuinely large. The risk isn't any one item; it's that launch keeps sliding while the list grows.

Why it bitesMomentum and morale are real assets at zero awareness. A launch that's "two weeks away" for three months is its own failure mode.
Warning signThe "before launch" list keeps getting longer, not shorter; new features get added before the paywall ships.
MoveRuthless sequencing: ship the smallest thing a stranger can pay for and keep (account + paywall + save), then launch. Everything else is post-launch. Resist polishing the free tool while the money path is still missing.
E · Legal, privacy & trust — the quiet ones that bite later
MedCollecting emails with no privacy policy / terms new

Your timeline lists "privacy policy + terms (before collecting emails)" — good that it's there, but it's a blocker, not a nice-to-have. The moment you capture an email you're processing personal data. With EU/UK visitors that means GDPR (lawful basis, consent, a way to delete). Once you take payment, you also need refund terms and tax handling.

Why it bitesA productivity tool for managers will get international traffic from day one (Product Hunt is global). "We'll add the policy later" means you were non-compliant for every signup in between.
Warning signEmail capture is live but the footer has no privacy link; no cookie/consent notice; no documented way to delete a user.
MoveShip privacy policy + terms + a basic consent notice in the same release as email capture, not after. A billing provider like Lemon Squeezy/Paddle can also act as merchant-of-record and absorb the sales-tax/VAT complexity.
LowClaims & benchmark substantiation new

Marketing copy that promises "reclaim X hours a week" is a results claim. If the benchmarks behind it aren't sourced and defensible, it's both a credibility risk (see "aha number") and, at scale, an advertising-claims risk.

Why it bitesLow now (small audience), but it compounds: the bigger your reach, the more a soft claim attracts skeptics and screenshots.
Warning signYou can't answer "where does the 14 hours come from?" in one sentence with a source.
MoveKeep a one-page source-of-truth for every benchmark. Phrase claims as "based on your inputs vs. role benchmarks" rather than absolute guarantees.
F · Competitive & market — slower-moving, watch don't panic
LowAn incumbent bolts on a "15-min diagnostic"

Your competitor analysis names this directly: Reclaim, Motion, or ClickUp could add a diagnostic to existing distribution and erase your one-liner overnight. They have the audience you'd be spending a year building.

Why it bitesDistribution beats features. They don't need a better engine, just a "good enough" version in front of users who already trust them.
Warning signAn incumbent ships a "productivity assessment" or "time audit" quiz.
MoveYour defense is depth-of-methodology (Oren) + the accumulating waste-pattern dataset, not the feature itself. Lean into the things a bolt-on can't copy: the ranked, role-specific fix plan and the coach relationship.
LowPaid ad CAC runs hotter than $29 LTV

B2B CPCs of $2–3 + a sub-3% conversion + month-1 churn can easily push customer-acquisition cost above what a $29/mo (often single-month) customer is worth. The brief's $75 CAC kill-switch is good — the risk is not honoring it.

Why it bitesUnit economics that don't close mean every paid dollar loses money — you'd be buying churn.
Warning signCAC > $75 after 4 weeks (your own gate), or LTV unknown because churn isn't measured yet.
MoveHonor the kill-switch. Don't scale paid until annual-plan take-rate or retention proves LTV > ~3× CAC. Until then, free channels only.

03 Fix-before-launch checklist

The subset of the above that must be true before you take a single dollar. If one of these is unchecked, you're not launch-ready — you're collecting IOUs.

  • blocker  A stranger can pay and instantly get access — accounts + checkout + paywall on /focus, zero manual steps from you.
  • blocker  A paid user's plan survives a browser clear and a device switch — server-side save, or at minimum an honest export + warning.
  • blocker  Privacy policy + terms + consent notice live in the same release as email capture.
  • The "aha number" shows its sources — benchmark each input is compared against is visible, so it reads as diagnosis not horoscope.
  • At least one recurring-value loop exists (re-assessment or progress nudge) before charging monthly — or lead with annual.
  • The paywall shows a teaser of the fix plan so value is visible before the wall.
  • Every channel has a kill-date and a success number, not just a "do it" checkbox.
  • The individual track works with zero involvement from Oren — no single-person dependency on revenue.
  • You can answer "where does the 14 hours come from?" in one sourced sentence.
  • The $75 CAC kill-switch is written down with who watches it and when.

05 If you fix only one thing

Close the money path before you open the traffic taps. Right now, even a perfect launch can't make revenue — there's no account, no checkout, no paywall, and a paying user's plan can vanish when they clear their browser. Driving traffic at that is pouring water into a bucket with no bottom. Build the smallest version of "a stranger pays and keeps what they paid for" first. Then — and only then — go make noise.

Everything else in this document is real, but sequenced behind that. Death A (silence) is loud and scary, so it gets all the attention — but Death B (leak) is the one you can fix this week with code, and it's the one quietly guaranteed by today's architecture.
