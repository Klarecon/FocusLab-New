1. Overview

The Focus Tool lives at /focus. It takes the waste sources identified by the Pareto Analyzer and helps users decide what to do about them.

It answers three questions:

1. Which fixes should I try?

The Assign Fixes tab shows research-backed solutions for each waste drain. Users pick the ones that fit their situation.

2. Which fixes give the most bang for the buck?

The EVI Matrix (Effort vs. Impact) plots every chosen fix on a scatter chart so users can see what's easy-and-impactful vs. hard-and-thankless.

3. How much time (and money) will I actually get back?

The Payoff tab runs a de-overlap engine that calculates realistic reclaim hours, annualizes them, and converts to dollars.

Tab 1: Assign Fixes

The user sees their waste sources grouped by zone:

  • Zone A (Vital Few) — the top ~20% of sources causing ~80% of waste. Always shown first.
  • Zone B (Useful Many) — smaller drains. Collapsible.

For each drain, the tool shows pre-built solutions from a catalog of ~90+ research-backed fixes (cited from HBR, McKinsey, Shopify, DORA, Gloria Mark, etc.). Users toggle solutions on/off. They can also add custom fixes.

Tab 2: EVI Matrix

A scatter chart where each chosen solution is a dot. Users can click any dot to re-score its effort and impact on a 1–5 scale, overriding catalog defaults.

Tab 3: Payoff

The de-overlap engine runs and displays: reclaimable hours per week, annualized hours, dollar value, before/after comparison, and cost of doing nothing.

4. Quick-Win Detection

Definition

Quick Win = effort ≤ 2 AND impact ≥ 4

Low effort, high impact. The fixes you should do first.

Solutions that qualify get a Quick Win badge in the UI. The payoff engine also caps quick-win reclaim at 60% of total waste — because even easy fixes have execution friction. A few simple changes can't credibly erase your entire waste load.

6. The De-Overlap Algorithm

Core Math — This is the most important piece of the methodology.

The Problem

If a user picks 3 solutions that all target "meetings," naive addition would claim total reclaim of 5 + 4 + 3 = 12 hrs/week — but the meeting drain is only 8 hrs/week. Double-counting destroys credibility.

The Solution: Residual-Based Compounding

The engine groups solutions by waste source, sorts by impact (highest first), then applies each solution's impact fraction to the remaining residual, not the original total.

Impact Fractions

// What % of a drain can one solution plausibly erase? IMPACT_FRACTION = { 1: 10%, // minimal 2: 20%, // modest 3: 35%, // about a third 4: 50%, // half 5: 60% // most, but never all }

The Algorithm Step by Step

// Step 1: Group solutions by waste source byBucket: Map<slug, ChosenSolution[]> // Step 2: Sort within each group by impact (highest first) ordered = sort by IMPACT_FRACTION descending // Step 3: Apply to residual, not total for each solution in ordered: take = IMPACT_FRACTION[impact] × residual creditByRow[rowId] = take residual -= take
Worked Example

Meeting drain: 8 hrs/week. Three solutions picked, sorted by impact:

SolutionImpactFractionTakes FromCreditedResidual After
Solution A560%8.00 hrs 4.80 hrs3.20 hrs
Solution B450%3.20 hrs 1.60 hrs1.60 hrs
Solution C220%1.60 hrs 0.32 hrs1.28 hrs

Total credited: 6.72 hrs/week (not 11.8 from naive addition).
1.28 hrs orphaned — intentionally. No plan is 100% effective.

This prevents the "stacking magic" trap: solutions compound, they don't combine linearly.

7. Payoff Calculation

Two Modes

Full Potential Aspirational

Sum of all credited reclaim from every chosen solution. Capped at total waste hours (can never exceed what you actually waste). This is the "if everything works" number.

Quick-Win Hours Conservative

Sum of reclaim from quick-win solutions only (effort ≤ 2, impact ≥ 4). Capped at 60% of total waste. This is the "what you'll credibly save soon" number.

9. Design Principles

  1. Pareto focus. 80% of waste comes from ~20% of sources (zones A + B). Don't waste time optimizing Zone C.
  2. Effort-impact tradeoff. Not all high-impact fixes are feasible. Low-effort wins deserve priority. The quadrant system guides triage.
  3. De-overlap honesty. Solutions compound, they don't stack. Residual tracking prevents math magic. Users trust the numbers because they're conservative.
  4. Quick-win reality check. Even low-effort solutions are capped at 60% of total waste. Execution friction is real.
  5. Conservative + aspirational split. Quick-win mode (what you'll save soon) vs. full potential (theoretical max). Both are useful; neither lies.
  6. Monetization for motivation. Hours feel abstract. Converting to yearly dollars and weeks of freed time makes the case visceral.
  7. Granular scoring. Effort (1–5) and impact (1–5) let users calibrate each fix to their context, not just catalog defaults.
  8. Research-backed. Every solution cites a real source (HBR, McKinsey, DORA, etc.). This isn't generic AI advice.
